✅ Unit 1: Social Media Analytics Basics
1Define Social Media Analytics & explain its need.10 MHOT
+

Social Media Analytics (SMA) is the process of collecting data from social media platforms (Facebook, Twitter/X, YouTube, blogs, forums), analyzing it using statistical and computational techniques, and converting it into actionable business insights.

Need / Importance:

  • Brand Monitoring: Track what customers are saying about your brand in real-time and catch PR crises early.
  • Customer Insights: Understand customer preferences, pain points, and sentiments directly from their own words.
  • Competitive Intelligence: Monitor competitor activities, campaigns, and audience reactions.
  • Marketing ROI: Measure the effectiveness of social media campaigns and optimize ad spend.
  • Product Improvement: Use customer feedback/reviews to improve existing products or develop new ones.
  • Trend Identification: Spot emerging trends, viral content, and shifting consumer behavior patterns.

4Challenges of Social Media Analytics.
+
  • Data Volume & Velocity: Millions of posts generated every minute; requires robust infrastructure to process in real-time.
  • Unstructured Data: Social media text contains slang, abbreviations, emojis, sarcasm, and multiple languages — making NLP extremely challenging.
  • Spam & Bot Detection: Fake accounts and bots skew analytics. Identifying and filtering them is crucial for accurate insights.
  • Privacy & Legal Issues: GDPR, platform Terms of Service, and ethical concerns around scraping personal user data.
  • Platform API Limitations: Rate limits, restricted access (Twitter/X now charges for API), and changing API structures.
  • Data Quality & Noise: Irrelevant posts, duplicate content, and incomplete data degrade analysis quality.
  • Measuring ROI: Difficult to directly link social media activity to actual revenue or conversions.
5Types of Social Media Analytics.
+
  • Descriptive Analytics: "What happened?" — Summarizes past social media activity. Example: Your Instagram post received 5,000 likes last week.
  • Diagnostic Analytics: "Why did it happen?" — Identifies causes behind outcomes. Example: The post went viral because a celebrity reshared it.
  • Predictive Analytics: "What will happen?" — Uses historical patterns to forecast future trends. Example: Based on past campaigns, launching on Friday evening will yield 30% more engagement.
  • Prescriptive Analytics: "What should we do?" — Recommends specific actions. Example: Increase ad spend on Instagram Reels by 25% and reduce Facebook banner ads.

Also classified by layer:

Text Analytics, Network Analytics, Action Analytics, Hyperlink Analytics, Location Analytics, Search Engine Analytics, Mobile Analytics — these are the Seven Layers of SMA.

✅ Unit 2: Social Network Analysis
6Explain Nodes, Edges, Degree, Centralization, Density.10 MHOT
+

Social Network Analysis (SNA) models social media as a mathematical graph G = (V, E) where V = set of vertices/nodes and E = set of edges/links.

  • Nodes (Vertices): Individual entities in the network — users, pages, groups, hashtags. Each node represents an actor.
  • Edges (Links/Ties): Connections between nodes — friendships, follows, retweets, comments. Can be directed (A follows B) or undirected (A and B are mutual friends).
  • Degree: The number of direct connections a node has. In-degree = incoming connections (followers). Out-degree = outgoing connections (following). High degree = popular/influential.
  • Centralization: Measures how much the network revolves around a single dominant node. A highly centralized network has one powerful hub (e.g., a celebrity account). A decentralized network distributes influence evenly.
  • Density: Ratio of actual connections to maximum possible connections. Formula: D = 2E / N(N-1) for undirected graphs. High density = tight-knit community where almost everyone knows everyone.
7Explain Network Visualization techniques.
+

Network Visualization is the graphical representation of social network data to reveal patterns, clusters, and key actors.

Common Layout Algorithms:

  • Force-Directed Layout (Fruchterman-Reingold): Nodes repel each other like charged particles, but edges act like springs pulling connected nodes together. Results in an intuitive, organic-looking graph.
  • Circular Layout: All nodes arranged in a circle; edges drawn as chords. Good for showing connection density patterns.
  • Hierarchical Layout: Tree-like structure showing parent-child relationships. Used for organizational charts or influence cascades.

Key Visual Features:

  • Node Size: Proportional to degree centrality (bigger = more connections)
  • Node Color: Represents community/cluster membership
  • Edge Thickness: Represents interaction frequency or tie strength

Scale Issues:

Large networks (millions of nodes) cause visual clutter. Solutions: sampling, filtering low-degree nodes, or using aggregation techniques.

8Types of Social Networks + Examples.
+
  • Directed Network: Edges have direction. User A follows User B, but B may not follow A back. Example: Twitter/X follower network.
  • Undirected Network: Edges are mutual/bidirectional. If A is connected to B, then B is also connected to A. Example: Facebook friendships, LinkedIn connections.
  • Weighted Network: Edges carry a numerical value representing strength of connection (e.g., number of messages exchanged). Example: Email communication networks.
  • Bipartite Network: Two distinct types of nodes where edges only connect nodes of different types. Example: Users connected to Products they reviewed on Amazon.
  • Ego Network: A subgraph centered on one specific node (ego) and all its direct connections (alters). Used to study an individual's immediate social sphere.
9Tie Strength & Trust in Social Networks.
+

Tie Strength (Granovetter's Theory):

  • Strong Ties: Close friends, family, frequent collaborators. High interaction frequency, emotional closeness, mutual trust. They provide emotional support but often share redundant information because they belong to the same circles.
  • Weak Ties: Acquaintances, distant contacts. Low interaction frequency. However, they are extremely valuable because they bridge different communities and bring novel, non-redundant information. This is Granovetter's famous "Strength of Weak Ties" theory.

Trust:

Trust in social networks determines how information flows and is received. Users are more likely to act on recommendations from trusted connections. Trust is influenced by:

  • Frequency of interaction
  • Reciprocity of engagement
  • Duration of the relationship
  • Shared community membership

✅ Unit 3: Text, Action & Hyperlink Analytics
11Explain Social Media Text Analytics with steps.10 MHOT
+

Social Media Text Analytics is the process of extracting meaningful information from unstructured text data generated on social media platforms using NLP techniques.

Steps in Text Analytics:

  1. Data Collection: Gather text data from social platforms using APIs (Twitter API returns JSON with tweet text, user info, timestamps) or scraping tools.
  2. Text Preprocessing/Cleaning:
    • Lowercasing all text
    • Removing URLs, @mentions, #hashtags, HTML tags
    • Removing special characters and numbers
    • Tokenization — splitting text into individual words/tokens
    • Stop-word removal — removing common words ("the", "is", "and")
    • Stemming/Lemmatization — reducing words to root form ("running" → "run")
  3. Feature Extraction: Convert text into numerical representations using TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) or Bag of Words (BoW) model.
  4. Analysis/Modeling: Apply NLP algorithms — Sentiment Analysis (VADER, TextBlob), Topic Modeling (LDA), Named Entity Recognition (NER), Trend Detection.
  5. Interpretation & Visualization: Generate word clouds, sentiment distribution charts, topic clusters, and present actionable insights.

Python Libraries Used:

NLTK, spaCy, TextBlob, scikit-learn, Gensim (for LDA), Transformers (BERT).

12Sentiment Analysis — concept + applications.⭐ HOT
+

Sentiment Analysis (also called Opinion Mining) is an NLP technique that identifies and classifies the emotional tone/polarity of text as Positive, Negative, or Neutral.

How It Works:

  • Lexicon-Based Approach: Uses a predefined dictionary of words with assigned sentiment scores. VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) is popular for social media — it handles emojis, capitalization ("AMAZING" > "amazing"), and slang.
  • Machine Learning Approach: Trains classifiers (Naive Bayes, SVM, Random Forest) on labeled datasets. More accurate but requires training data.
  • Deep Learning Approach: Uses pre-trained transformer models like BERT that understand context deeply. Best at handling sarcasm and nuanced language.

Business Applications:

  • Brand Health Monitoring: Tracking overall sentiment about your brand over time.
  • Product Launch Analysis: Gauging public reaction to a new product release.
  • Crisis Detection: Detecting sudden negative sentiment spikes indicating a PR crisis.
  • Customer Service Prioritization: Auto-routing highly negative complaints to priority support.
  • Political Analysis: Measuring public opinion on election candidates from Twitter data.

14Hyperlink Analytics — types + importance.
+

Hyperlink Analytics analyzes the linking patterns between web pages, social media profiles, and online content to measure influence, authority, and information flow.

Types of Hyperlinks:

  • In-links (Backlinks): Links pointing TO your page from external sources. More high-quality in-links = higher domain authority.
  • Out-links: Links from your page pointing to external websites.
  • Self-links (Internal Links): Links within the same website/domain connecting different pages.

Importance:

  • SEO Ranking: Google's PageRank algorithm ranks pages based on the quality and quantity of in-links.
  • Influence Mapping: Identifying which social media content or influencers drive the most traffic via link sharing.
  • Information Flow Analysis: Understanding how content spreads across the web and social networks through shared links.

Tools:

Ahrefs, Moz Link Explorer, Google Search Console, VOSON (for academic hyperlink analysis).

15Types of Social Media Text (structured/unstructured).
+
  • Microblog Text: Short, character-limited posts (Twitter/X: 280 chars). Contains heavy slang, abbreviations, hashtags, emojis. Requires specialized NLP handling.
  • Long-Form Text: Blog posts (Medium, WordPress), forum discussions (Reddit threads), detailed product reviews (Amazon). Richer vocabulary, better grammar, more context for analysis.
  • Conversational Text: Comments, replies, chat messages. Often informal, context-dependent, and threaded.
  • User-Generated Content (UGC): Reviews, testimonials, wiki contributions. Provides authentic consumer voice data.

From a Data Structure perspective:

  • Structured: Metadata like timestamps, user IDs, follower counts — stored in tables.
  • Semi-structured: JSON/XML from APIs — has some schema but flexible fields.
  • Unstructured: The actual text content, images, videos — no predefined format.
✅ Unit 4: Location & Search Engine Analytics
16Explain Location Analytics + sources of location data.
+

Location Analytics involves analyzing geographic/geospatial data attached to social media posts to extract location-based business insights.

Sources of Location Data:

  • GPS Coordinates: Directly from mobile devices when users enable location services (latitude/longitude embedded in posts).
  • Geotags: User-added location tags on posts (e.g., "📍 Mumbai, India" on Instagram).
  • Check-ins: Explicit location sharing (Facebook Check-in, Foursquare/Swarm).
  • IP Address Geolocation: Approximate user location derived from their internet connection IP.
  • Text Mentions: NLP-based extraction of place names from post text ("Just landed in Delhi!").
  • Photo EXIF Data: GPS coordinates embedded in photo metadata.

Business Applications:

Targeted local advertising, store location planning, event monitoring, tourism trend analysis, disaster response tracking.

17Privacy concerns in Location Analytics.⭐ HOT
+
  • User Unawareness: Many users don't realize their posts contain embedded GPS data or that location can be inferred from metadata.
  • Stalking & Physical Safety: Real-time or historical location data can be exploited for stalking, burglary, or harassment.
  • Data Aggregation Risks: Even anonymized location data can be de-anonymized by combining multiple data points to identify movement patterns unique to an individual.
  • GDPR Compliance: Under GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), location data is classified as personal data. Collecting it without explicit user consent is illegal in the EU.
  • Third-Party Data Sharing: Platforms may share or sell location data to advertisers or data brokers without clear user knowledge.
  • Government Surveillance: Governments may access location data for mass surveillance purposes, raising civil liberties concerns.

Mitigation:

Data anonymization, opt-in consent mechanisms, data minimization (collect only what's needed), transparent privacy policies, and encryption of location records.

✅ Unit 5: Information Filtering & KPIs
20Recommendation Systems — types + working.10 M⭐ HOT
+

Recommendation Systems are automated information filtering engines that predict and suggest items a user might be interested in, based on data patterns.

Types:

  • Content-Based Filtering: Recommends items similar in attributes to what the user previously liked. Analyzes item features (genre, tags, keywords). Example: "You watched an action movie → here's another action movie." Limitation: Can't suggest novel items outside user's existing taste.
  • Collaborative Filtering: Based on collective behavior of similar users.
    • User-Based: "Users similar to you also liked X."
    • Item-Based: "Users who liked item A also liked item B."
    Limitation: Cold Start Problem — fails for new users with no history.
  • Hybrid Systems: Combine both content-based and collaborative approaches to overcome individual limitations. Netflix and Amazon use hybrid systems. Solves the Cold Start problem by using content features for new users until enough interaction data accumulates.

Real-World Examples:

Netflix (movie recommendations), Amazon (product suggestions), Spotify (Discover Weekly), YouTube (video suggestions), LinkedIn (job recommendations).

23Social Media Strategy formulation.
+

Steps to Formulate a Social Media Strategy:

  1. Set Clear Objectives: Define SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). Example: "Increase Instagram engagement by 25% in 3 months."
  2. Identify Target Audience: Define demographics (age, gender, location), psychographics (interests, values), and which platforms they use (GenZ → TikTok/Insta, B2B → LinkedIn).
  3. Conduct Competitive Analysis: Analyze competitor content, posting frequency, engagement rates, and audience sentiment.
  4. Choose Platforms & Content Mix: Select platforms aligned with audience. Plan content types (videos, carousels, stories, blogs) and voice/tone.
  5. Create Content Calendar: Schedule posts with optimal timing based on audience activity data.
  6. Define KPIs: Set measurable metrics (engagement rate, CTR, conversion rate) to track success.
  7. Execute, Monitor & Optimize: Deploy content, track performance using analytics tools, and continuously refine strategy based on data.
24Social Media Risks in business.
+
  • Reputation Damage: A single viral negative post or employee blunder can destroy years of brand building overnight.
  • Data Breaches: Hacked social accounts expose customer data, business secrets, and damage trust (e.g., Cambridge Analytica scandal).
  • Misinformation & Fake News: False information about a brand can spread rapidly and is difficult to contain.
  • Legal & Compliance Risks: Violating advertising standards, copyright infringement, GDPR violations when handling user data.
  • Negative Viral Content: Customer complaints or boycott campaigns going viral, leading to financial loss.

Mitigation:

Social media policy for employees, crisis communication plan, real-time sentiment monitoring, legal review of campaigns, and regular security audits.

27Privacy policies, data ownership & issues.⭐ HOT
+

Every social media platform has a privacy policy and Terms of Service (ToS) that governs how user data is collected, stored, shared, and used. Users agree to these when signing up.

  • De-anonymization: "Anonymous" data can be re-identified by combining multiple datasets.

29Steps of Social Media Data Collection (scraping, crawling, parsing).⭐ HOT
+

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Identify Data Requirements: Define what data is needed (tweets, comments, reviews, user profiles) and from which platform (Twitter, Facebook, YouTube, Reddit).
  2. Choose Collection Method:
    • API-Based: Using official platform APIs (Twitter API v2, Facebook Graph API, YouTube Data API). Returns structured JSON data. Most reliable and ethical method.
    • Web Scraping: Using Python libraries (BeautifulSoup for static HTML, Selenium for JavaScript-rendered pages) to extract data directly from web pages. Used when no API is available.
    • Web Crawling: Automated bots that systematically browse and index web pages by following hyperlinks. Tools: Scrapy (Python framework).
  3. Authentication: For API access — register a developer app, obtain API keys/tokens, configure OAuth 2.0 authentication.
  4. Data Extraction & Parsing: Parse the collected raw data (HTML/XML/JSON). Extract specific fields like tweet text, timestamp, user_id, likes count. Python's `json` library for API responses; `BeautifulSoup` for HTML parsing.
  5. Handle Rate Limits: APIs enforce rate limits (e.g., 300 requests per 15 minutes). Implement pagination and retry logic with delays.
  6. Store Raw Data: Save collected data into MongoDB (for unstructured JSON), CSV files, or data lakes for subsequent processing.

Tools:

Python (Tweepy for Twitter, PRAW for Reddit), BeautifulSoup, Scrapy, Selenium, Postman (for API testing).

30Data Cleaning & Storage using Python/MongoDB.
+

Data Cleaning Pipeline (Python):

  1. Remove Duplicates: Using Pandas df.drop_duplicates() to eliminate repeated posts/tweets.
  2. Handle Missing Values: Drop rows with null critical fields or fill with defaults using df.fillna().
  3. Text Cleaning: Using Regular Expressions (Regex) — re.sub() to remove URLs, @mentions, special characters, HTML tags, and extra whitespace.
  4. Lowercasing: text.lower() for uniformity.
  5. Tokenization: NLTK's word_tokenize() splits text into individual words.
  6. Stop Word Removal: NLTK's stopwords corpus filters out low-value words.
  7. Lemmatization: NLTK's WordNetLemmatizer() reduces words to dictionary base form ("better" → "good").

Storage in MongoDB:

  • MongoDB stores data as BSON documents (Binary JSON) in collections.
  • Python connects via pymongo library: client = MongoClient('localhost', 27017)
  • Each tweet/post becomes a document: db.tweets.insert_one({text: "...", sentiment: "positive", user: "...", timestamp: "..."})
  • Why MongoDB? Schema-less design handles variable social media data structures perfectly. Easy to scale horizontally.

32Content-based Social Media Analytics model (sentiment, topic).⭐ HOT
+

Content-based models analyze the actual content (text, images, audio, video) shared on social media rather than the network structure.

Key Techniques:

  • Sentiment Analysis: Classify text as Positive/Negative/Neutral using VADER (for social media slang + emojis) or TextBlob. For higher accuracy, fine-tune BERT on labeled social media datasets.
  • Topic Modeling (LDA): Latent Dirichlet Allocation discovers hidden topics in a large corpus. Feed 50,000 tweets → LDA might discover Topic 1: {"battery", "drain", "charge"}, Topic 2: {"camera", "photo", "quality"}.
  • Trend/Issue Detection: Monitor sudden spikes in keyword/hashtag frequency to identify emerging trends or issues before they go viral.
  • Emoji/Emoticon Analysis: Map emojis to sentiment values (😀 = positive, 😡 = negative). Emojis often carry more genuine sentiment than text.
  • Image Analytics: Using CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks) or pre-trained models (ResNet, YOLO) to detect objects, logos, faces, or inappropriate content in shared images.
  • Video/Audio Analytics: Speech-to-text conversion followed by text analysis. Video frame analysis for brand logo detection or scene understanding.

Business Use:

A restaurant chain can run sentiment analysis on Zomato/Google reviews, use topic modeling to discover top complaints (food quality vs service speed vs hygiene), and take targeted corrective action.

33Structure-based model (community detection, influence analysis).
+

Structure-based models analyze the network topology — the pattern of connections and relationships between users — rather than the content they share.

Community Detection:

Algorithms that partition the network graph into densely connected subgroups (communities) where members interact more with each other than with outsiders.

  • Louvain Algorithm: Optimizes modularity score iteratively. Fast and scalable for large networks. Groups nodes into communities where intra-community edges are dense.
  • Girvan-Newman Algorithm: Removes edges with highest betweenness centrality iteratively, naturally splitting the network into communities.
  • Modularity (Q): A score (0 to 1) measuring the quality of community division. Higher Q = better-defined communities.

Influence Analysis:

Identifying the most influential nodes/users in the network using centrality measures:

  • Degree Centrality: Number of direct connections. High = popular.
  • Betweenness Centrality: Users who bridge different communities. They control information flow.
  • Closeness Centrality: Users who can reach everyone else quickly. Best for spreading viral content.
  • PageRank: Google's algorithm applied to social networks. Nodes linked by other important nodes get higher scores.

Tools:

NetworkX (Python), Gephi, igraph (R).

A real-time social media dashboard provides live, continuously updating visualizations of social media metrics to enable instant decision-making.

Architecture:

  1. Data Ingestion: Connect to platform streaming APIs (Twitter Filtered Stream, YouTube Live Chat). Data arrives as continuous JSON stream.

  • Trending hashtags/keywords leaderboard
  • Geographic heatmap of post locations
  • Engagement metrics KPI cards (likes, shares, comments per minute)
  • Historical trend line for comparison
35Competitor analysis using social media data.⭐ HOT
+

Competitor Analysis on social media involves systematically monitoring and analyzing competitor performance, strategies, and audience perception to identify opportunities and threats.

Steps:

  1. Identify Competitors: Direct competitors (same product/market) and indirect competitors (substitute products). Set up monitoring for their social profiles.
  2. Track Key Metrics:
    • Share of Voice (SOV): What % of total industry conversations mention each competitor? Formula: (Brand mentions / Total industry mentions) × 100
    • Engagement Rate: Compare likes, comments, shares per post across competitors.
    • Follower Growth Rate: Who is growing faster and why?
    • Post Frequency & Timing: When and how often do competitors post?
  3. Content Strategy Analysis: What type of content gets the most engagement for competitors? (Videos vs images vs text, tone of voice, hashtag strategy)
  4. Sentiment Analysis on Competitor Mentions: Run NLP on comments/reviews about competitors to discover what customers love/hate about them. Customer pain points = YOUR opportunity.
  5. SWOT Analysis: Compile findings into Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats.

Tools:

Sprout Social, Hootsuite, SEMrush Social, BrandWatch, SimilarWeb, Social Blade (YouTube), and custom Python scripts using APIs.
